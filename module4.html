Tab 1 — Foundations
What is Hedging?
A hedge is a position in derivatives that offsets the risk of an existing or anticipated exposure in the physical (spot) market. The goal is risk reduction, not profit maximisation. A perfect hedge eliminates all risk; in practice, basis risk always remains.
📉
Short Hedge
Used by someone who owns an asset or will sell it in the future. Sell futures today to lock in a selling price. Protects against falling prices.

Examples: Gold miner, wheat farmer, oil producer, equity investor.
Action: SELL futures → Locks in sale price
📈
Long Hedge
Used by someone who will buy an asset in the future. Buy futures today to lock in a purchase price. Protects against rising prices.

Examples: Airline buying jet fuel, jeweller buying gold, importer buying USD.
Action: BUY futures → Locks in purchase price
⚖️
Perfect vs Imperfect Hedge
A perfect hedge fully eliminates risk — same asset, same quantity, same expiry. An imperfect hedge has basis risk — arises when hedging with a different asset (cross-hedge), different quantity, or different timing.
Perfect hedge P&L = 0 regardless of price
📏
Basis Risk
Basis = Spot Price − Futures Price. In a perfect hedge, basis converges to zero at expiry. Basis strengthens when spot rises relative to futures. Basis weakens when spot falls relative to futures.
Basis = S − F → 0 at expiry (convergence)
🔢
Minimum Variance Hedge Ratio
Optimal fraction of exposure to hedge. Not always 1:1. Depends on correlation and relative volatility of spot vs futures.

h* = ρ × (σ_S / σ_F)
N* = h* × Q_A / Q_F (optimal contracts)
🧩
Cross-Hedge
Hedging an asset with a futures contract on a different but correlated asset. Example: Hedge jet fuel (no listed futures) using crude oil futures (correlated). Cross-hedges carry higher basis risk than direct hedges.
e.g., Jet fuel ↔ Crude oil (ρ ≈ 0.93)

📉 Short Hedge — Worked Example
Setup: GoldMine India will produce 10,000 oz of gold in 3 months. Current spot = ₹71,200/10g (approx ₹7,12,000/oz MCX equivalent). MCX Gold futures (3-month) = ₹7,25,000/oz. Lot = 1 oz. Fear: gold price falls.
Action todaySELL 10,000 gold futures at ₹7,25,000
Scenario A: Gold falls to ₹6,80,000Futures gain = ₹45,000 × 10,000 = ₹45 Cr
Spot revenue loss₹(7,12,000 − 6,80,000)×10,000 = ₹32 Cr lost
Net effective price₹7,25,000/oz (locked in) ✅
Scenario B: Gold rises to ₹7,60,000Futures loss = ₹35,000 × 10,000 = ₹35 Cr
Spot revenue gain₹4.8 Cr extra revenue
Net effective priceStill ~₹7,25,000/oz ✅
📈 Long Hedge — Worked Example
Setup: SpiceJet will buy 2,00,000 barrels of crude oil in 3 months. Current crude spot = ₹6,850/bbl. MCX Crude futures (3-month) = ₹6,920/bbl. Lot = 100 barrels. Fear: crude price rises.
Action todayBUY 2,000 crude futures at ₹6,920
Scenario A: Crude rises to ₹7,800Futures gain = ₹880 × 2L = ₹17.6 Cr
Extra purchase cost (spot)₹(7,800−6,850)×2L = ₹19 Cr extra
Net effective purchase price₹6,920/bbl (locked in) ✅
Scenario B: Crude falls to ₹5,900Futures loss = ₹1,020 × 2L = ₹20.4 Cr
Spot purchase saving₹19 Cr cheaper
Net effective purchase priceStill ~₹6,920/bbl ✅
Key Insight: In both cases, the futures gain/loss nearly offsets the spot loss/gain, locking in a price close to the original futures price. The hedge "works" in both directions — it caps losses AND forgoes windfall gains. That is exactly the point: certainty over speculation.

Tab 3 — Basis Risk Simulator
Understanding Basis Risk
Basis = Spot − Futures. In a perfect world it converges to zero at expiry. In practice, spot and futures don't move in perfect lockstep — the difference (basis) fluctuates, creating residual risk even after hedging.
📐
Basis Definition
Basis = Spot Price − Futures Price

Opening basis: known at hedge initiation.
Closing basis: uncertain — this is basis risk!

For commodities: Basis typically negative (futures > spot = normal contango).
b₁ = S₁ − F₁ (opening) · b₂ = S₂ − F₂ (closing)
💪
Strengthening Basis
Basis becomes less negative (or more positive) — spot rises relative to futures. Short hedgers benefit from strengthening basis. Long hedgers are hurt.

Example: Opens at −50, closes at −20 → Strengthened by 30.
Good for short hedger (seller)
💫
Weakening Basis
Basis becomes more negative (or less positive) — spot falls relative to futures. Long hedgers benefit from weakening basis. Short hedgers are hurt.

Example: Opens at −50, closes at −80 → Weakened by 30.
Good for long hedger (buyer)

Tab 4 — Minimum Variance Hedge Ratio
Optimal Hedge Ratio
The minimum variance hedge ratio (h*) is the fraction of exposure to hedge with futures to minimise the variance (risk) of the hedged position. It is determined by the correlation and relative volatility of the spot and futures prices.
MINIMUM VARIANCE HEDGE RATIO
h* = ρ × (σS / σF)
Number of contracts: N* = h* × QA / QF
ρ = correlation(ΔS, ΔF) · σS = std dev of spot price changes · σF = std dev of futures price changes

Tab 5 — Equity Portfolio Risk Management
Hedging a Stock Portfolio with Nifty Futures
A fund manager holding a portfolio of Indian stocks can use Nifty 50 futures to hedge systematic (market) risk. The number of contracts depends on the portfolio beta — a measure of sensitivity to market moves.
NIFTY HEDGE FORMULA
N* = (βtarget − βportfolio) × P / (F × QF)
P = portfolio value · F = Nifty futures price · Q_F = lot size · β_target = desired beta (0 = fully hedged)
