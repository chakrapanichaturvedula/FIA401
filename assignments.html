Rajesh holds a short position in 4 Nifty futures contracts (lot size = 50 units each). He entered the position when Nifty was at 22,100. At expiry, Nifty settles at 22,350. Calculate his total P&L (positive = profit, negative = loss).

Total P&L (₹)
Step-by-step:

Total P&L = −12,500 × 4 = −₹50,000
A1.2 5 pts

An Indian exporter expects USD 500,000 in 3 months. She sells USD/INR futures at 84.20. At expiry, USD/INR settles at 83.50. Each contract is for USD 1,000. How many contracts did she need, and what is her net hedge P&L in rupees? Enter only the hedge P&L (positive = gain).

Hedge P&L (₹)
Step-by-step:
Number of contracts = 500,000 / 1,000 = 500 contracts
She is short futures; rupee appreciated (USD/INR fell) → short position gains
Gain per USD = 84.20 − 83.50 = ₹0.70
Total hedge P&L = 0.70 × 500,000 = +₹350,000
A1.3 5 pts

A cash-and-carry arbitrage opportunity: Nifty spot = 22,000. 3-month futures trade at 22,450. Risk-free rate = 7% p.a., no dividends. Calculate the fair value futures price (F*) using continuous compounding and state whether the actual price suggests overpricing (enter 1) or underpricing (enter −1). Enter just F* rounded to the nearest integer.

F* (₹, nearest integer)
Step-by-step:
F* = S × e^{rT} = 22,000 × e^{0.07 × 0.25}
= 22,000 × e^{0.0175}
= 22,000 × 1.01765 ≈ 22,389 ≈ 22,390

Actual futures (22,450) > F* (22,390) → Futures are overpriced
Arbitrage: Buy spot Nifty, sell futures at 22,450 → lock in riskless ≈60 pt gain.

Futures margin problem: Priya buys 3 Nifty futures contracts (lot = 50) at 22,200. Initial margin = ₹1,00,000 per contract; maintenance margin = ₹75,000 per contract. On Day 1, Nifty settles at 22,050. What is the MTM loss and does Priya receive a margin call? Enter the Day 1 MTM loss as a positive number.

Day 1 MTM loss (₹)
Step-by-step:
Price move = 22,050 − 22,200 = −150 points
MTM loss = 150 × 50 × 3 = ₹22,500

Account balance = 3,00,000 − 22,500 = ₹2,77,500

A2.2 5 pts

Minimum variance hedge ratio: A fund manager holds a ₹8 crore equity portfolio (β = 1.4). Nifty futures are at 22,500 (lot = 50). How many futures contracts should he short to fully hedge the portfolio (round to nearest integer)?

Futures contracts to short
Step-by-step:
N* = β × (Portfolio Value / Futures Contract Value)
Futures contract value = 22,500 × 50 = ₹11,25,000
N* = 1.4 × (8,00,00,000 / 11,25,000)
= 1.4 × 71.11
= 99.56 ≈ 100 contracts (accept 99–101)
A2.3 5 pts

Currency futures hedge: An importer must pay EUR 2,50,000 in 2 months. EUR/INR futures price = 92.50 (contract size = EUR 1,000). He buys futures to lock in the rate. At settlement, EUR/INR = 93.80. What is his effective exchange rate paid per EUR (net of hedge gain)?

Effective rate (₹ per EUR)
Step-by-step:
Market rate paid = ₹93.80/EUR
Futures gain (long position, rate rose) = 93.80 − 92.50 = ₹1.30/EUR
Effective rate = 93.80 − 1.30 = ₹92.50/EUR

This is exactly the locked-in futures rate — a perfect hedge.

A3.2 5 pts

Put-call parity check: A European call on Infosys (K = ₹1,800, T = 3 months, r = 6.5% p.a.) trades at ₹95. Infosys spot = ₹1,820. Using put-call parity, calculate the fair value of the corresponding European put (in ₹, round to 2 decimal places).

Put fair value (₹)
Step-by-step:
P = C + K·e^{−rT} − S
K·e^{−rT} = 1800 × e^{−0.065 × 0.25} = 1800 × e^{−0.01625}
= 1800 × 0.98389 = ₹1,771.00
P = 95 + 1,771.00 − 1,820 = ₹46.00 (accept 45–47)
A3.3 5 pts

Risk-neutral pricing (binomial): A stock trades at ₹500. In one month it can rise to ₹560 or fall to ₹450. Risk-free rate = 6% p.a. (continuous). A call option has strike K = ₹510. Calculate the risk-neutral probability p of an up-move (round to 4 decimal places).

Risk-neutral probability p
Step-by-step:
u = 560/500 = 1.12   d = 450/500 = 0.90
e^{rT} = e^{0.06/12} = e^{0.005} ≈ 1.005013
p = (e^{rT} − d) / (u − d)
= (1.005013 − 0.90) / (1.12 − 0.90)
= 0.105013 / 0.22
= 0.4773 (accept 0.472–0.482)

A4.1 5 pts

Delta estimate: A Reliance put option has Δ = −0.38. Reliance rises by ₹45. Using delta alone, estimate the change in the put's value (positive = gain, negative = loss).

ΔP (₹)
Step-by-step:
ΔP ≈ Δ × ΔS
ΔP ≈ −0.38 × 45 = −₹17.10

The put loses ₹17.10 in value as the stock rises — the negative delta tells us the put moves in the opposite direction to the underlying.
A4.2 5 pts

Vega-theta trade-off: A long straddle on Nifty has aggregate Vega = ₹1,200 and Theta = −₹350/day. If IV rises from 16% to 19% over 4 trading days (no spot change), what is the net change in the straddle's value?

Net ΔV (₹)
Step-by-step:
IV change = 19% − 16% = 3%
Vega gain = 1,200 × 3 = +₹3,600
Theta loss = 350 × 4 = −₹1,400
Net ΔV = 3,600 − 1,400 = +₹2,200
A4.3 5 pts

Gamma-delta neutral: A portfolio has Δ = −500 and Γ = +1,200. A traded call option has Δ = 0.55 and Γ = 0.018 per share (each contract = 100 shares). First, how many contracts must you add to neutralise gamma? Enter the number of contracts to the nearest integer (positive = buy, negative = sell).

Option contracts (+ buy / − sell)
Step-by-step:
w* = −Γportfolio / Γoption per contract
Γ per contract = 0.018 × 100 shares = 1.8
w* = −1,200 / 1.8 = −666.7 ≈ −667 contracts

Sell 667 contracts of the call to neutralise gamma.
Then rebalance delta using the underlying/futures.
