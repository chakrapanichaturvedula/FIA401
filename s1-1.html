Derivative Basics

Markets, Longs & Shorts

What derivatives are, where they trade, and the two fundamental positions every trader takes.

What is a Derivative?

A derivative is a financial contract whose value derives from the performance of an underlying asset, index, or rate. The underlying can be an equity (Reliance, Nifty), commodity (gold, crude oil), currency (USD/INR), or interest rate.

Derivatives are not investments in the underlying itself — they are agreements about the future value of that underlying. This distinction gives derivatives their power: you can take a large economic exposure with a fraction of the capital.

Key idea: If you buy 100 shares of Reliance at ₹2,450, you own ₹2,45,000 worth of stock. If you buy one Reliance futures contract (lot size 250), you control ₹6,12,500 of exposure with an initial margin of roughly ₹60,000 — about 10% of the notional.
Futures
Agreement to buy/sell an asset at a fixed price on a fixed future date. Traded on exchange, daily MTM.
Options
Right (but not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) at a strike price before or on expiry.
Swaps
Exchange of cash flows over time — e.g. fixed interest rate for floating rate (interest rate swap).

Exchange-Traded vs OTC Markets

Derivatives trade in two distinct arenas, each with its own risk profile and regulation.

FeatureExchange-TradedOTC (Over-the-Counter)
Trading venueNSE / BSE (India), CME (US)Bilateral agreement between two parties

SettlementDaily MTM + central clearingAt maturity (usually)

Central Clearing: Exchange-traded derivatives eliminate counterparty risk through a central counterparty (CCP). In India, the NSE Clearing Ltd (NSCCL) stands between every buyer and seller. If one party defaults, the CCP covers the loss from the margin pool.

Long & Short Positions

Every derivative trade involves exactly two sides — a long and a short. Understanding which side you are on, and how you profit or lose, is the foundation of all further analysis.

📈 Long Position
You buy (or agree to buy) the asset. You profit when the price rises. You lose when the price falls.

Examples: Buying a call option. Taking the buy side of a futures contract. Buying shares.
📉 Short Position
You sell (or agree to sell) the asset. You profit when the price falls. You lose when the price rises.

Examples: Writing a call option. Taking the sell side of a futures contract. Short-selling shares.

Payoff at Expiry

Long futures payoff  = FT − F0
Short futures payoff = F0 − FT

where F0 = entry price, FT = closing/expiry price

P&L = (FT − F0) × lot size × contracts

Zero-sum game: The long's gain is exactly equal to the short's loss and vice versa. Derivatives do not create wealth — they transfer risk from those who don't want it (hedgers) to those who do (speculators).

Worked Example

Scenario: Arjun is bullish on Nifty. He buys one Nifty50 futures contract at ₹22,000. The lot size is 50 units.

Notice that if Arjun had simply bought 50 shares of the Nifty ETF at ₹220 each (≈ ₹11,000 outlay), his upside would be the same but he would have needed much more capital. The futures allowed him to take the same economic position with lower upfront capital.

Practice Problem

Problem 1.1: Priya shorts one Nifty50 futures contract at ₹21,800 (lot size = 50). The contract closes at expiry at ₹21,600. What is her total P&L on the position (in ₹)?

Enter your answer (positive = profit, negative = loss):

P&L (₹)
Step-by-step solution:
Short P&L = (F0 − FT) × lot size
= (21,800 − 21,600) × 50
= 200 × 50
= ₹10,000 profit

Priya sold at 21,800 and the price fell to 21,600 — the short position gained ₹200 per unit × 50 units.
