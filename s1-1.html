What is a Derivative?

A derivative is a financial contract whose value derives from the performance of an underlying asset, index, or rate. The underlying can be an equity (Reliance, Nifty), commodity (gold, crude oil), currency (USD/INR), or interest rate.

Derivatives are not investments in the underlying itself - they are agreements about the future value of that underlying. This distinction gives derivatives their power: you can take a large economic exposure with a fraction of the capital.

Key idea: If you buy 100 shares of Reliance at Rs 2,450, you own Rs 2,45,000 worth of stock. If you buy one Reliance futures contract (lot size 250), you control Rs 6,12,500 of exposure with an initial margin of roughly Rs 60,000 - about 10% of the notional.
Futures
Agreement to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price on a fixed future date. Traded on exchange, daily MTM.
Options
Right, but not obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) at a strike price before or on expiry.
Swaps
Exchange of cash flows over time, for example fixed interest rate for floating rate.

Exchange-Traded vs OTC Markets

Derivatives trade in two distinct arenas, each with its own risk profile and regulation.

FeatureExchange-TradedOTC (Over-the-Counter)
Trading venueNSE / BSE (India), CME (US)Bilateral agreement between two parties
Contract designStandardised (lot size, expiry)Fully customised
SettlementDaily MTM + central clearingAt maturity (usually)
Counterparty riskEliminated - NSCCL guaranteesHigh - depends on credit of counterparty
TransparencyPublic prices, volumes, OIPrivate - reported post-trade
LiquidityHigh for major contractsDepends on relationship

Central Clearing: Exchange-traded derivatives eliminate counterparty risk through a central counterparty (CCP). In India, the NSE Clearing Ltd (NSCCL) stands between every buyer and seller. If one party defaults, the CCP covers the loss from the margin pool.

Long and Short Positions

Every derivative trade involves exactly two sides - a long and a short. Understanding which side you are on, and how you profit or lose, is the foundation of all further analysis.

Long Position
You buy (or agree to buy) the asset. You profit when the price rises. You lose when the price falls.

Examples: Buying a call option. Taking the buy side of a futures contract. Buying shares.
Short Position
You sell (or agree to sell) the asset. You profit when the price falls. You lose when the price rises.

Examples: Writing a call option. Taking the sell side of a futures contract. Short-selling shares.

Payoff at Expiry

Long futures payoff = FT - F0
Short futures payoff = F0 - FT

where F0 = entry price, FT = closing or expiry price

22,500+Rs 25,000-Rs 25,000
23,000+Rs 50,000-Rs 50,000

P&L = (FT - F0) x lot size x contracts

Zero-sum game: The long's gain is exactly equal to the short's loss and vice versa. Derivatives do not create wealth - they transfer risk from those who do not want it to those who do.

Worked Example

Scenario: Arjun is bullish on Nifty. He buys one Nifty50 futures contract at Rs 22,000. The lot size is 50 units.

Outcome at ExpiryNifty closes atArjun's P&LWhat happened
Bullish view correct22,350+Rs 17,500(22,350 - 22,000) x 50
Sideways22,000Rs 0No movement
Bearish move21,700-Rs 15,000(21,700 - 22,000) x 50

Notice that if Arjun had simply bought the underlying exposure directly, he would have needed much more capital. The futures allowed him to take the same economic view with a lower upfront outlay.

Practice Problem

Problem 1.1: Priya shorts one Nifty50 futures contract at Rs 21,800 (lot size = 50). The contract closes at expiry at Rs 21,600. What is her total P&L on the position (in Rs)?

Enter your answer (positive = profit, negative = loss):

P&L (Rs)
Step-by-step solution:
Short P&L = (F0 - FT) x lot size
= (21,800 - 21,600) x 50
= 200 x 50
= Rs 10,000 profit

Priya sold at 21,800 and the price fell to 21,600, so the short position gained Rs 200 per unit x 50 units.
