Currency Futures

FX exposure types, interest rate parity pricing, and executing hedges with USDINR futures.

Foreign Exchange Exposure

Indian companies routinely face FX exposure — uncertainty about the INR value of foreign-currency cash flows. This exposure can be a risk to manage (hedger) or an opportunity to exploit (speculator).

Transaction Exposure
Known future cash flows in a foreign currency. Examples: Import payment due in 90 days, export invoice receivable in USD.
Translation Exposure
Accounting effect of converting foreign subsidiary's financials into INR. Affects the P&L and balance sheet but may not represent real cash flows.
Economic Exposure
Long-run impact of exchange rate changes on competitive position. An IT firm competing with US peers faces economic exposure if USD weakens.

For this course, we focus on transaction exposure — the most direct and easiest to hedge with futures.

Currency Futures Pricing: Covered Interest Rate Parity

Currency futures are priced by the principle of Covered Interest Rate Parity (CIRP). If you can earn rd at home or rf abroad, the futures rate must satisfy:

FFX = S · e(rd − rf)T

where: S = spot rate (INR per USD), rd = INR risk-free rate,
rf = USD risk-free rate, T = time to expiry (years)
Intuition: If Indian rates are higher than US rates (rd > rf), the rupee is expected to depreciate — so the futures USD/INR rate is higher than the spot rate. INR is "cheaper in the future" because you could earn more by holding rupees at home.

Worked Example: Pricing USDINR Futures

InputValue
Spot USD/INR (S)₹83.50
INR risk-free rate (rd)6.8% p.a.
USD risk-free rate (rf)5.3% p.a.

Executing a Currency Hedge

The direction of the hedge depends on your underlying exposure:

PartyUnderlying ExposureFutures ActionHedge Type
Exporter (IT company) Will receive USD → long USD Sell USD futures (short) Short hedge
Importer (oil company) Must pay USD → short USD Buy USD futures (long) Long hedge
USD borrower USD loan → short USD Buy USD futures Long hedge
USD lender USD investment → long USD Sell USD futures Short hedge

Number of Contracts

N = Exposure (USD) ÷ Contract size (USD per contract)

NSE USDINR contract size = USD 1,000
Monthly expiry, cash-settled in INR

Full Hedge Example: Indian Importer

Scenario: An Indian oil company must pay USD 5,00,000 in 3 months for crude oil. Current spot = ₹83.50. 3-month futures = ₹84.20 (in contango).

StepActionDetail
Now Buy 500 USDINR futures at ₹84.20 N = 5,00,000 ÷ 1,000 = 500 contracts
At expiry (spot = 85.00) Futures converge to ₹85.00 Gain = (85.00 − 84.20) × 1,000 × 500 = ₹4,00,000
Import cost Pay ₹85.00 per USD in spot market Extra cost vs budget = (85.00 − 83.50) × 5,00,000 = ₹75,00,000 more
Net outcome Futures gain offsets part of extra cost Effective rate ≈ ₹84.20/USD (locked-in futures rate)
Basis risk here: The company budgeted at the futures rate (₹84.20), but the actual effective rate may differ slightly because the futures may not perfectly converge to the exact spot rate used in the import contract. This small residual is basis risk.

Basis Risk in Currency Hedging

A hedge is rarely perfect. Basis risk arises from mismatches between the futures contract and the actual exposure:

  • Maturity mismatch: The futures expiry doesn't exactly match the payment date.
  • Size mismatch: Contract size may not match exposure exactly (rounding to whole contracts).
  • Currency mismatch: Hedging EUR exposure with USD contracts (cross-hedge).
  • Daily settlement: The futures produces daily cash flows while the underlying transaction settles at one point — this creates reinvestment uncertainty.

Practice Problem

Problem 2.3: An IT exporter has USD 2,00,000 receivable in 2 months. She sells 200 USDINR futures contracts at ₹83.80. At expiry, the spot rate is ₹83.00. What is the total gain on her short hedge (in ₹)?

Gain = (Fsold − FT) × 1,000 × N

Gain (₹)
Step-by-step:
Sold at 83.80, settled at 83.00
Gain per USD = 83.80 − 83.00 = ₹0.80
Each contract = 1,000 USD
Total gain = 0.80 × 1,000 × 200 = ₹1,60,000

This offsets the lower rupee value of her export proceeds (which decreased because USD weakened).
