Historical vs Implied Volatility

There are two fundamentally different ways to measure volatility. They tell different stories about the same underlying asset.

Historical (Realised) Volatility
Computed from past price returns. Standard deviation of log-returns, annualised.

Formula: σ_hist = std(ln(S_t/S_{t−1})) × √252

Tells you what volatility was. Backward-looking.
Implied Volatility (IV)
Extracted from current market prices. The σ that, when plugged into BSM, yields the observed market premium.

Method: Solve BSM for σ given C, S, K, r, T.

Tells you what the market expects. Forward-looking.
IV as market fear gauge: India's VIX (India Volatility Index) is based on Nifty option IVs. When VIX spikes (e.g., elections, COVID crash), option premiums surge — the market is pricing in more uncertainty. Traders say "IV is cheap" or "IV is expensive" relative to historical vol.

Computing Historical Volatility (Step-by-Step)

Step 1: Compute daily log-returns: r_t = ln(S_t / S_{t−1})
Step 2: Compute mean: r̄ = (1/n) Σ r_t
Step 3: Daily variance: s² = Σ(r_t − r̄)² / (n−1)
Step 4: Daily volatility: s = √s²
Step 5: Annualise: σ_hist = s × √252    (252 trading days)

The Volatility Smile and Skew

If BSM were perfectly correct, options at all strikes for the same expiry would have the same implied volatility. In reality, IVs vary by strike, creating characteristic patterns:

Volatility Smile (FX Markets)
IV is higher for both deep ITM and deep OTM options, lower near ATM. Creates a "U-shape" or smile. Reflects fat tails — large moves occur more often than log-normal predicts.
Volatility Skew (Equity Markets)
IV is higher for OTM puts than OTM calls at same distance from ATM. Creates a downward-sloping "smirk." Reflects crash risk premium — investors pay more for downside protection. Common in Nifty/Sensex options.

Put-Call Parity

Put-call parity is a fundamental no-arbitrage relationship between call and put prices with the same underlying, strike, and expiry:

C − P = S · e−qT − K · e−rT    (with dividend yield q)

Simpler form (no dividends): C + K·e−rT = P + S

If violated: arbitrage exists via "conversion" or "reversal" trade.

Intuition: Synthetic Positions

Synthetic PositionComponentsEquivalent to
Synthetic long stockLong call + Short put (same K, T)Holding the stock
Synthetic long callLong put + Long stock + Borrow K·e^−rTLong call
Synthetic long putLong call + Short stock + Lend K·e^−rTLong put
Practical use: If SEBI bans short selling in a stock, you can create a "synthetic short" using options (long put + short call + lend PV of K). Put-call parity ensures the economics are equivalent. Market makers use this constantly.

Practice Problem

Problem 3.4: Nifty call (K = 22,000, T = 1 month) = ₹215. Risk-free rate = 6%, no dividends. S = ₹22,000. Using put-call parity, what should the put price be? (Round to nearest rupee.)

P = C + K·e^−rT − S

Put price (₹)
Step-by-step:
T = 1/12 year
K·e^−rT = 22,000 × e^(−0.06 × 1/12) = 22,000 × e^−0.005 = 22,000 × 0.99501 = 21,890
P = C + K·e^−rT − S = 215 + 21,890 − 22,000 = ₹105

If the actual put were trading at ₹120, it would be overpriced. An arbitrageur would sell the put, buy the call, buy the risk-free bond (PV of K), and short the stock.

Quick Check

1. Implied volatility (IV) is best described as:

  • The standard deviation of past price returns
  • The GARCH forecast of future volatility
  • The volatility that makes the BSM price equal to the observed market option price
  • The maximum historical drawdown expressed as a percentage

2. The volatility skew in equity option markets (like Nifty) shows that:

  • ATM options have the highest implied volatility
  • OTM calls are more expensive than OTM puts
  • OTM puts have higher IV than OTM calls at the same distance from ATM
  • IV is constant across all strikes

3. Put-call parity implies that for European options with same K and T:

  • Call price always equals put price
  • Call price always exceeds put price
  • C + K·e^−rT = P + S (no-arbitrage relationship)
  • Call delta + Put delta = 0
