Theta, Vega & Rho

Time decay, volatility sensitivity, and interest rate sensitivity — the remaining dimensions of option risk.

Theta (Θ): Time Decay

Theta measures how much the option's value decreases as one day passes, all else held equal. It represents the "rent" paid for holding the option — the cost of time.

Θ = ∂C / ∂t    (usually reported per day or per year)

BSM (Call, with dividend yield q):
Θcall = −S·σ·e−qT·N'(d₁)/(2√T) − r·K·e−rT·N(d₂) + q·S·e−qT·N(d₁)

Typically reported as: ΔC per day = Θ / 365
Long Options: Negative Theta
Option buyers lose time value every day. An ATM Nifty call might lose ₹8–12 per day from theta decay alone, even if Nifty doesn't move.
Short Options: Positive Theta
Option sellers earn theta daily — time value flows to them as the option's time premium erodes. This is the "income" from selling straddles or covered calls.

Theta Acceleration Near Expiry

Theta is not constant — it accelerates as expiry approaches, especially for ATM options. Deep OTM options lose most of their value in the final weeks as the last shred of time value evaporates.

Days to ExpiryATM Call Theta (illustrative)Observation

Gamma-Theta duality: High gamma = high theta cost. ATM options near expiry have maximum gamma (convexity) but also maximum theta decay. Option sellers exploit theta but are exposed to gamma risk. You cannot have gamma without theta.

Vega (ν): Volatility Sensitivity

Vega measures how much an option's price changes for a 1% change in implied volatility. Vega is not a Greek letter — it's a made-up name, but it is universally used in the industry.

ν = ∂C / ∂σ = ∂P / ∂σ

BSM: ν = S · e−qT · N'(d₁) · √T

Vega is identical for calls and puts (same K, T, S)
Reported as: ₹ change per 1% rise in IV
Long Vega (Option Buyers)
All long option positions have positive vega. When IV rises, options become more valuable. Straddle buyers are "long vega" — they profit when fear spikes.
Short Vega (Option Sellers)
All short option positions have negative vega. When IV spikes (market panic), short option positions suffer significant mark-to-market losses even if the stock barely moves.
ScenarioVegaTypical magnitude
ATM option, long expiryMaximumLarge (e.g. ₹80 per 1% IV change for 3-month ATM)
OTM optionModerateLower than ATM
Near expiry (any strike)Near zeroIV changes have little effect — time almost gone
IV regime matters: Buying options when IV is historically low and selling when IV is high is a popular volatility trading strategy. The India VIX hovering at 12 vs spiking to 30 makes a huge difference to vega P&L for option portfolios.

Rho (ρ): Interest Rate Sensitivity

Rho measures an option's sensitivity to changes in the risk-free interest rate. It is typically the least important Greek for short-term equity options, but matters more for long-dated options and interest rate derivatives.

ρcall = K · T · e−rT · N(d₂)  > 0    (Calls benefit from rate rises)
ρput = −K · T · e−rT · N(−d₂) < 0    (Puts suffer from rate rises)

Intuition: Higher rates make it cheaper to synthetically own the underlying (less cost of carry), increasing call value. They also reduce the PV of the strike payment, which hurts puts. For long-dated options (LEAPS), rho can be substantial.

Practice Problem

Problem 4.2: A call option has Vega = ₹85 (per 1% change in IV) and Theta = −₹12/day. If implied volatility rises by 2% and 3 days pass with no change in spot, what is the net estimated change in the call's value?

Net ΔC (₹)
Step-by-step:
Vega gain = ν × Δσ = 85 × 2 = +₹170
Theta loss = Θ × days = −12 × 3 = −₹36
Net ΔC = 170 − 36 = +₹134

The IV spike more than offset 3 days of time decay — the call gained ₹134 in value.

Quick Check

1. A trader who is long a straddle has:

  • Positive theta (earns from time decay)
  • Negative theta (loses time value each day)
  • Zero theta (calls and puts cancel)
  • Theta that depends on whether Nifty is rising

2. Vega is generally highest for:

  • Deep OTM options with 1 week to expiry
  • Deep ITM options near expiry
  • ATM options with long time to expiry
  • Deep ITM options with long time to expiry

3. When interest rates rise (all else equal), the value of a European call option:

  • Decreases (higher discount rate reduces all future payoffs)
  • Stays the same (interest rates don't affect options)
  • Increases (higher rates lower the PV of the strike payment)
  • Could go either way depending on dividend yield
