The Option Greeks
Greeks measure the sensitivity of an option's price to changes in market variables. Every options desk tracks these continuously — they are the language of risk management for structured products and derivatives portfolios.
Δ
Delta FIRST ORDER
Rate of change of option price with respect to the underlying asset price. Tells you: for every ₹1 move in S, the option moves ₹Δ.
Call: Δ = N(d₁) Put: Δ = N(d₁) − 1 where d₁ = [ln(S/K)+(r+σ²/2)T] / σ√T
Call Δ: 0 to 1 · Put Δ: −1 to 0 · ATM ≈ ±0.5
Γ
Gamma SECOND ORDER
Rate of change of Delta with respect to spot price. Measures the curvature or convexity of the option's value. High Γ = Delta changes rapidly.
Γ = N'(d₁) / (S · σ · √T) where N'(x) = (1/√2π)·e^(−x²/2)
Always positive (calls & puts) · Max at ATM · Spikes near expiry
Θ
Theta TIME DECAY
Rate of change of option price with respect to time (passage of one day). Usually negative — options lose value as expiry approaches, holding all else constant.
Call: Θ = −[S·N'(d₁)·σ/(2√T)] − r·K·e^(−rT)·N(d₂) Per day: divide by 365
Usually negative · Long option: pays theta · Short option: earns theta
ν
Vega VOLATILITY
Rate of change of option price with respect to implied volatility. Vega is NOT a Greek letter — it's a made-up symbol. A 1% rise in σ changes option value by ν.
ν = S · √T · N'(d₁) (same for calls and puts)
Always positive (calls & puts) · Long vol = long vega · Max ATM
ρ
Rho INTEREST RATE
Rate of change of option price with respect to the risk-free interest rate. Long-dated options have larger rho. Less important in short-term trading.
Call: ρ = K·T·e^(−rT)·N(d₂) Put: ρ = −K·T·e^(−rT)·N(−d₂)
Call ρ > 0 · Put ρ < 0 · Larger for longer maturities
Greeks Intuition — Key Relationships
⚖️
Delta Neutrality
A delta-neutral portfolio has Δ = 0 — it doesn't move with small changes in S. Market makers constantly rebalance to delta-neutral. Example: Hold 1 ATM call (Δ=0.5) + sell 50 shares per 100 options to neutralise.
Delta hedge: N_shares = −Δ_option × N_options
📈
Gamma–Theta Trade-off
Long options have positive Gamma (good — profits accelerate) but negative Theta (bad — time decay costs). Short options: collect Theta but exposed to Gamma. This is the central trade-off in options market-making.
Gamma P&L ≈ ½ · Γ · (ΔS)² · dt
🌊
Vega & Implied Vol
Implied volatility is the market's expectation of future vol, backed out from option prices using BSM. When IV rises, all long option positions gain (positive vega). "Long vol" means you profit from uncertainty rising.
Option price ≈ BSM(σ_implied)
🔗
Put-Call Parity & Greeks
From put-call parity (C − P = S − Ke^(−rT)), the Greeks are related: Δ_call − Δ_put = 1. Γ_call = Γ_put. ν_call = ν_put. Only Θ and ρ differ between calls and puts.
Δ_C − Δ_P = 1 (always true)
🏁
Near-Expiry Behaviour
As T→0, ITM options approach Δ=1 (call) or Δ=−1 (put). ATM options have explosive Gamma. Theta peaks. Vega → 0. This is why near-expiry ATM options are most sensitive to small moves — the famous "gamma scalping" zone.
T→0: Γ → ∞ (ATM), Θ → ∞, ν → 0
📊
BSM PDE from Greeks
The BSM equation links all Greeks: Θ + rS·Δ + ½σ²S²·Γ = r·V. This says the option's time value plus weighted Delta and Gamma must equal the risk-free return on the option's value. Every hedged portfolio satisfies this.
Θ + rS·Δ + ½σ²S²·Γ = rV

Exotic Derivatives
Exotic options have payoffs that differ from standard European/American options. They are tailored instruments — designed for specific hedging needs, structured products, or to reduce premium cost by accepting more complex conditions.
Why Exotics Exist
Standard (vanilla) options are often too expensive or imprecisely calibrated for corporate hedging needs. Exotic options are engineered to: (1) reduce upfront premium, (2) match specific cash flow patterns, (3) hedge path-dependent risks. Investment banks structure and sell exotics to corporates; understanding them is essential for CO4 (instrument design).
🚧
Barrier Options
PATH-DEPENDENT · HULL CH. 25
MOST COMMON EXOTIC
Barrier options activate (knock-in) or terminate (knock-out) if the underlying price crosses a specified barrier level during the option's life. The payoff depends on whether the barrier is touched at any point — making these path-dependent.
Types: • Up-and-Out Call: Standard call that EXPIRES if S rises above barrier H • Up-and-In Call: Call that only ACTIVATES if S rises above H • Down-and-Out Put: Standard put that EXPIRES if S falls below H • Down-and-In Put: Put that only ACTIVATES if S falls below H Key pricing identity: [Knock-In Option] + [Knock-Out Option] = [Vanilla Option] Example: Up-and-Out Call (S₀=1285, K=1300, H=1500, T=3mo) → Premium ≈ 40% cheaper than vanilla call → Buyer gives up: option dies if Reliance rallies past ₹1500
📌 Indian Corporate Use: An exporter receives USD. They buy a Down-and-In USD put (barrier = USD/INR 80). If USD falls below 80, the put activates and they're protected. If USD stays above 80, the put never kicks in — they paid a lower premium for "conditional" protection. This is cheaper than a vanilla put.
📊
Asian Options (Average Price Options)
AVERAGING · PATH-DEPENDENT
POPULAR IN COMMODITIES
The payoff depends on the average price of the underlying over the option's life, not just the final price. This makes them less susceptible to price manipulation at expiry and better suited to hedging companies with ongoing exposure (e.g., an oil refinery buying crude oil every month).
Average Price Call: max(S_avg − K, 0) Average Price Put: max(K − S_avg, 0) Average Strike Call: max(S_T − S_avg, 0) S_avg = arithmetic or geometric average of prices over T Example: Indian oil refinery imports crude oil monthly for 12 months. Vanilla call: pays off if oil price high on single expiry date. Asian call: pays off based on AVERAGE monthly price over the year. → Better matches actual cost exposure → Typically 20–30% cheaper than vanilla due to lower volatility of averages
📌 Why cheaper? The average of a variable is less volatile than the variable itself. Lower volatility → lower option price (recall Vega: ν > 0). Asian options suit any company that buys/sells the underlying continuously rather than at one point.
🔭
Lookback Options
OPTIMAL EXERCISE · PATH-DEPENDENT
MOST EXPENSIVE EXOTIC
Lookback options give the holder the benefit of the optimal entry or exit point over the option's life — in retrospect. You could have "looked back" and chosen the best moment to buy or sell.
Lookback Call (fixed strike): max(S_max − K, 0) → Pays based on the MAXIMUM price reached during T Lookback Put (fixed strike): max(K − S_min, 0) → Pays based on the MINIMUM price reached during T Floating Strike Lookback Call: S_T − S_min → Buyer always gets the "best" entry point in retrospect Cost: Most expensive exotic — premium can be 2–3× a vanilla option
📌 Why so expensive? The holder is guaranteed to exercise at the optimal time — the maximum or minimum price over the entire period. This eliminates market timing risk entirely. Investment banks use Monte Carlo simulation to price lookbacks because no simple closed-form solution exists (unlike Asian options).
🔌
Binary (Digital) Options
ALL-OR-NOTHING · DISCONTINUOUS PAYOFF
HIGH DELTA RISK
Binary options pay a fixed amount (Q) if a condition is met, or nothing if not. The payoff is discontinuous — zero or fixed — making them "all-or-nothing" bets. They are popular in structured retail products and embedded in corporate bonds.
Cash-or-Nothing Call: Q × 1{S_T > K} → Pays Q if S_T > K, else 0 Asset-or-Nothing Call: S_T × 1{S_T > K} → Pays S_T if S_T > K, else 0 Relationship: Vanilla Call = Asset-or-Nothing Call − K × Cash-or-Nothing Call Pricing (BSM formula): Cash-or-Nothing Call = Q × e^(−rT) × N(d₂) Value of d₂: probability (risk-neutral) of finishing in-the-money
📌 Risk Warning: Near expiry when S ≈ K, the Delta of a binary option can become extremely large (infinite theoretical Delta at S=K, T=0). This makes binary options near expiry almost impossible to delta-hedge. Market makers face severe "pin risk" when the underlying settles right at the strike on expiry day.
🪆
Compound Options
OPTIONS ON OPTIONS
COMPLEX STRUCTURE
A compound option is an option on another option — giving the right to buy or sell an option at a future date. There are four types: call-on-call, call-on-put, put-on-call, put-on-put. They are useful when you're uncertain whether you'll need an option at all.
Call-on-Call: Right to BUY a call option at time T₁ for premium x → Exercise if at T₁: call value > x Call-on-Put: Right to BUY a put option at time T₁ for premium x Two exercise dates: T₁ (compound) and T₂ > T₁ (underlying option) Two strikes: x (compound premium) and K (underlying option strike) Use case: Pharma company awaiting FDA approval. If approved, needs options to hedge FX risk. Buy a call-on-call now → minimal upfront cost. If approval fails → compound option expires worthless (small loss)
📌 Application: An Indian IT company bidding for a US government contract has FX exposure only if they win the bid (uncertain). They buy a compound option on a USD put — if they win the bid, they exercise the compound option to obtain the put and hedge; if they lose, the compound option expires for a small premium. Far cheaper than buying a full vanilla put upfront on uncertain exposure.

Monte Carlo Simulation
Monte Carlo simulation prices options by simulating thousands of random paths of the underlying asset under Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM), computing the payoff on each path, and averaging (then discounting). It is the only tractable method for path-dependent exotics.
GBM — The Price Path Model
dS = μ · S · dt + σ · S · dW
μ = drift (≈ r in risk-neutral) · σ = vol · dW = Wiener process increment
S(t+dt) = S(t) · exp[(r − ½σ²)dt + σ·√dt·Z]
Z ~ N(0,1) drawn fresh each step
Risk-neutral driftμ = r − q (q = div yield)
Volatility termσ · √dt · Z
Log-normal propertyln(S_T/S_0) ~ N(·)
Number of steps per pathT/dt (e.g. 252 for daily)
MC Pricing Algorithm
1
Simulate N paths of S using GBM from S₀ to S_T under risk-neutral measure (use r as drift)
2
For each path i, compute payoff: Vᵢ = max(Sᵢ_T − K, 0) for a call
3
Average the payoffs: V_bar = (1/N) × Σ Vᵢ
4
Discount to present: C = e^(−rT) × V_bar
5
Standard error = σ_payoff / √N. More paths → lower error. Convergence rate: 1/√N

Why Monte Carlo?
BSM gives a closed-form for European vanilla options. But for path-dependent exotics (Asian, Barrier, Lookback), no simple formula exists. Monte Carlo simulates the path and computes the payoff — it can handle any payoff function.
Pricing rule: E[payoff] · e^(-rT)
📉
Variance Reduction
Standard MC converges slowly (error ∝ 1/√N — need 4× paths to halve error). Techniques: Antithetic variates, Control variates, Stratified sampling, Quasi-random sequences (Sobol). Production-grade systems use these to achieve 1/N convergence.
Antithetic: pair (Z, -Z) each path
🏛️
Industry Applications
Monte Carlo is used to price: exotic equity options, mortgage-backed securities, credit derivatives (CDO tranches), interest rate exotics (Bermudan swaptions). Every major derivatives desk runs MC engines with millions of paths per pricing query.
N = 1M paths → Wall Street standard
