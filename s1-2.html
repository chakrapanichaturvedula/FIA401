Trader Types

Derivative markets work because different participants come in with different motives: reducing risk, taking risk, or exploiting price gaps. This lesson helps students identify the hat behind the trade.

Lesson Goal Separate hedging, speculation, and arbitrage as distinct economic roles instead of treating every trade as simple betting.
By the End You should be able to classify market participants and explain why their incentives shape derivatives pricing and liquidity.

What this lesson covers

This page introduces the three main trader types, shows how each uses derivatives differently, and closes with a short problem plus concept checks.

  • Why people enter derivative markets at all
  • Hedgers and the logic of risk transfer
  • Speculators and leveraged directional views
  • Arbitrageurs and pricing discipline

Why this matters

Once students can identify the trader's motive, later topics such as hedging strategy, market microstructure, and price discovery become much easier to interpret.

Why Do People Trade Derivatives?

Not everyone enters a derivative market for the same reason. Hull groups participants into three broad categories, each performing a different economic function. Before judging whether a trade is sensible, it helps to ask what problem the trader is trying to solve.

A corporate treasurer protecting cash flows, a trader taking a directional view, and a desk exploiting temporary mispricing may all use the same futures contract, but they are not playing the same game.

The three roles: Hedgers transfer risk they do not want. Speculators absorb that risk in exchange for expected profit. Arbitrageurs keep related prices aligned by trading away mispricing.

Hedgers

A hedger already has exposure through the real world: inventory, sales receipts, imports, debt, or investments. The derivative position is not the main business decision. It is a risk-management overlay designed to reduce uncertainty.

Short Hedge
Used when you already own the underlying or expect to receive it later and fear a price fall.

Example: A wheat farmer sells wheat futures before harvest to lock in a sale price.
Long Hedge
Used when you will need to buy the underlying later and fear a price rise.

Example: An importer buys USD futures to lock in the exchange rate for a future payment.

FX Hedging Example

An exporter expects USD 10,00,000 in three months. If USD/INR is 83.50 today but falls to 82.00 by settlement, the rupee value of export receipts declines materially. Selling USD futures today locks in the exchange rate and reduces that uncertainty.

The futures gain when USD/INR falls offsets the weaker rupee inflow from exports. The hedge may not maximize profit, but it protects planning certainty.

Hedging is about reducing risk, not necessarily achieving the best ex post outcome. If USD instead rises sharply, the exporter gives up upside in exchange for certainty.

Speculators

A speculator has no offsetting underlying exposure. They deliberately take on market risk because they believe they have an informational or analytical edge. Their payoff depends on being right about direction, volatility, or relative value.

Derivatives are attractive for speculation because they are leveraged. A relatively small margin deposit can control a large notional position, which magnifies both gains and losses.

Leverage cuts both ways: If a trader controls Rs 11 lakh of futures exposure with Rs 1.1 lakh margin, a 5 percent favorable move can generate a large return on margin. The same 5 percent adverse move can damage the account just as quickly.

Arbitrageurs

An arbitrageur looks for pricing inconsistencies between two related instruments. Instead of betting on a direction, they build a package trade that locks in a spread when one leg is too expensive and the other is too cheap.

Classic Futures Arbitrage
If the observed futures price F is above its theoretical fair value F* = S x e(r-q)T, sell the rich futures and buy the spot asset while financing the carry. At expiry, the spread closes and the arbitrage profit is captured.

If F is below F*, reverse the package: buy futures and short the spot asset.

In liquid markets these opportunities disappear quickly because arbitrage desks react immediately. Their activity is part of why cost-of-carry relations hold so well in active contracts.

Activity Recap: Which Hat Are You Wearing?

The same market move can mean three very different things depending on the trader. A futures loss can be acceptable for a hedger if the physical position gains. A speculator has no such offset. An arbitrageur cares only about the spread between related prices.

  • The hedger values stability more than maximum upside.
  • The speculator earns only if the market view is right.
  • The arbitrageur acts when prices violate a no-arbitrage relationship.
Before analysing any derivative position, first identify the trader's hat. That one step often tells you what a good outcome actually means.

Practice Problem

Problem 1.2: An Indian software exporter expects USD 5,00,000 in three months. She sells USD futures at Rs 83.50 per USD. At maturity, the spot and futures converge to Rs 82.80. What is her gain per USD on the futures position?

Hint: She sold first at 83.50 and closes effectively at 82.80.

Gain per USD (Rs)
Step-by-step solution:
Gain per USD = Fsold - FT
= 83.50 - 82.80 = Rs 0.70 per USD

Total gain = 0.70 x 5,00,000 = Rs 3,50,000.
This futures gain helps offset the lower rupee value of the export receipts.

Key Lens

A derivative trade only makes sense in context. Always ask whether the trader is transferring risk, taking risk, or harvesting mispricing.
