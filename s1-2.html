Derivative Basics

Trader Types

Hedgers, speculators, and arbitrageurs — three roles that make derivative markets function.

Why Do People Trade Derivatives?

Not everyone enters a derivative market for the same reason. Hull identifies three broad categories of participants, each playing a distinct economic role. Understanding your own role in any trade — and your counterparty's likely role — is the first step toward sound risk management.

The Three Roles: Hedgers transfer risk they don't want. Speculators absorb that risk in exchange for potential profit. Arbitrageurs police prices to keep markets consistent. All three roles are necessary for well-functioning markets.

Hedgers

A hedger already has an underlying exposure — through their business operations, investments, or financial obligations. They use derivatives to reduce or eliminate that exposure. The derivative gain offsets the underlying loss (or vice versa).

Short Hedge (Sell Futures)
Used when you own the underlying and fear a price drop.

Example: An Indian wheat farmer fears falling wheat prices at harvest. He sells wheat futures to lock in the current price.
Long Hedge (Buy Futures)
Used when you need to buy the underlying later and fear a price rise.

Example: An Indian importer must pay USD 5 lakh in 3 months. She buys USD futures now to lock in the exchange rate.

FX Hedging Example (Exporter)

An IT company expects USD 10,00,000 in export proceeds in 3 months. Current USD/INR = 83.50. If the rupee appreciates to 82.00 by then, the company receives ₹82,00,000 instead of ₹83,50,000 — a loss of ₹15,00,000.

By selling USD futures at 83.50, a rise in INR (fall in USD/INR) produces a gain on the futures that offsets the revenue loss. The company has locked in ₹83,50,000 regardless of spot rate movements.

Hedging does not always produce a better outcome than not hedging. If the USD strengthens to 85.00, the exporter misses out on extra rupees. Hedging is about certainty, not maximising return.

Speculators

A speculator has no underlying exposure. They take a position in derivatives based on a directional view — a forecast about future price movements — and bear risk deliberately in exchange for the chance of profit.

Derivatives are a natural tool for speculation because of leverage: a small margin deposit controls a large notional position.

Arbitrageurs

An arbitrageur exploits pricing inconsistencies between related markets. By simultaneously buying the cheap instrument and selling the expensive one, they lock in a riskless profit with no net investment.

Classic Futures Arbitrage
If the observed futures price F exceeds the theoretical fair price F* = S·e^(r−q)T:
→ Sell the overpriced futures + Buy the spot asset + Finance at r
→ At expiry, deliver the asset against the short futures → Riskless profit = F − F*

If F < F*, reverse the trade: Buy futures + Short spot + Lend proceeds.

In practice, arbitrageurs trade so quickly and in such volume that mispricing windows close within milliseconds on electronic exchanges. Their activity is why the cost-of-carry model holds in liquid markets.

Activity Recap: Two Worlds, Three Hats

In our in-class activity, you wore one of three "hats" — Hedger, Speculator, or Arbitrageur — and responded to the same price shock differently. Key insights from that exercise:

  • The hedger celebrated a futures loss because their physical inventory gained.
  • The speculator who guessed wrong on direction simply lost money and closed out.
  • The arbitrageur moved swiftly when the two prices diverged, pocketing the spread.
The same price movement has opposite implications for different trader types. Before analysing any trade, always ask: "Which hat am I wearing?"

Practice Problem

Problem 1.2: An Indian software exporter expects USD 5,00,000 in 3 months. She sells USD futures at an equivalent rate of ₹83.50/USD. At maturity, the spot rate is ₹82.80/USD. What is her approximate gain per USD on the futures position (in ₹)?

Hint: She sold USD at 83.50 (futures) and the futures converge to 82.80 at expiry.

Gain per USD (₹)
Step-by-step solution:
Gain per USD = Fsold − FT
= 83.50 − 82.80 = ₹0.70 per USD

She sold USD futures at 83.50. When USD/INR fell to 82.80, buying back (closing) was cheaper → she kept the ₹0.70 spread.
Total gain = 0.70 × 5,00,000 = ₹3,50,000
This partially offsets the lower rupee value of her export receipts.

Key Insight

Every derivatives position must have a counterparty. A hedger's short futures position is typically taken on by a speculator on the other side — this is risk transfer in action.
