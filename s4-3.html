Delta Neutrality

Constructing and maintaining a delta-neutral portfolio, dynamic rebalancing, and what market makers do every day.

What is Delta Neutrality?

A delta-neutral portfolio has a total delta of zero — meaning small changes in the underlying price have (almost) no effect on the portfolio's value. The portfolio is insensitive to the direction of the market, but still exposed to gamma, theta, and vega.

Who uses delta neutrality? Option market makers delta-hedge constantly — they take whatever position is needed to fill client orders, then immediately offset the directional risk with futures or shares. Their profit comes from the bid-ask spread, not from predicting market direction.
Portfolio Δ = Σ (position_i × Δ_i)

For delta neutral: Portfolio Δ = 0

Hedge shares = −(option delta × number of option contracts × lot size)

Building a Delta-Neutral Hedge

Step-by-Step Example: Market Maker Hedging a Call Sale

Scenario: A market maker sells 10 Reliance call contracts (lot size = 250, Δ = 0.55) to a client. She must hedge immediately.

StepCalculationAction
1. Compute net delta of options position Short 10 calls × 250 lots × Δ 0.55 = Short 1,375 delta Option position has −1,375 delta
2. Offset with underlying Buy 1,375 shares of Reliance (or 5.5 futures contracts) Long 1,375 delta from shares
3. Net portfolio delta −1,375 + 1,375 = 0 Delta neutral ✓
But Reliance moves! An hour later, Reliance rises ₹20. The call delta shifts from 0.55 to 0.62 (gamma effect). The portfolio is no longer delta neutral. The market maker must buy more shares — and this continuous rebalancing is called dynamic delta hedging.

Dynamic Delta Hedging

Because delta changes as the underlying moves (due to gamma), a one-time hedge is not enough. A delta-neutral portfolio requires continuous rebalancing — buying more when the stock rises and selling when it falls.

The hedger is buying high and selling low to maintain delta neutrality. These rebalancing trades cost money — this cost is the "gamma cost." It's exactly what the short option seller loses to the long option buyer's convexity advantage.

The Black-Scholes insight: If you delta-hedge continuously and the realised volatility equals the implied volatility embedded in the option price, the total hedging costs will exactly equal the option premium collected. This is how BSM pricing is derived via the replication argument.

Portfolio Greeks: Making a Portfolio Delta AND Gamma Neutral

Delta neutrality only protects against small moves. For large moves, you also need to neutralise gamma. However, you cannot make a portfolio both delta and gamma neutral using only the underlying — you need another option.

To make a portfolio gamma neutral:
Add options: w* = −Γportfolio / Γoption contracts

Then rebalance delta to zero using the underlying.

Order of operations: 1) Gamma neutral with options → 2) Delta neutral with futures/shares

Worked Example: Gamma-Delta Neutral Portfolio

Given: Portfolio Δ = +600, Γ = −800. A traded call option has Δ = 0.7, Γ = 0.015 per contract (each contract = 100 shares).

StepCalculationResult
1. Gamma neutralise w* = −(−800)/0.015/100 = 800/1.5 Buy 533 contracts of the call
2. New portfolio delta 600 + 533×100×0.7 = 600 + 37,310 Δ = +37,910
3. Delta neutralise Short 37,910 shares (or equivalent futures) Portfolio Δ = 0, Γ = 0

Practice Problem

Problem 4.3: You are short 5 Nifty call contracts (lot size = 50). The call delta is 0.45. How many Nifty futures contracts (lot = 50) must you buy to achieve delta neutrality?

Hint: Short calls give you negative delta. Buying futures gives positive delta.

Futures contracts to buy
Step-by-step:
Short 5 call contracts × lot 50 × Δ 0.45 = −112.5 delta
Need +112.5 delta from futures
Each futures contract = 1 × 50 = 50 delta
Contracts needed = 112.5 / 50 = 2.25 contracts

In practice, you'd round to 2 contracts, accepting a small residual delta.

Quick Check

1. A delta-neutral portfolio is protected against:

  • All market risk (delta, gamma, vega)
  • Volatility risk (vega) only
  • Small changes in the underlying price (but not large moves)
  • Time decay (theta) only

2. In dynamic delta hedging, the market maker who sold an option and delta-hedges continuously:

  • Always profits from the hedge
  • Makes zero profit (pure service)
  • Incurs hedging costs from gamma — buying high and selling low as they rebalance
  • Earns free theta without any gamma risk

3. To make a portfolio both delta-neutral AND gamma-neutral, you must:

  • Only trade the underlying asset
  • Sell options to reduce gamma, then sell futures to reduce delta
  • First add options to neutralise gamma, then use the underlying to neutralise delta
  • Buy equal numbers of calls and puts at the same strike
