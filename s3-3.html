Option Pricing

From the binomial tree to Black-Scholes — the mathematics of fair option value.

The Core Pricing Intuition

An option's fair price is the cost of a replicating portfolio — a combination of the underlying asset and a risk-free bond that produces exactly the same payoffs as the option in all future states. Because two identical payoff streams must sell at the same price (no-arbitrage), the option's price equals the replicating portfolio's cost.

No-arbitrage pricing: We don't need to know investors' risk preferences to price an option. We only need to know that no free lunch exists — if the option were mispriced, arbitrageurs would exploit the gap immediately, restoring fair value.

Binomial Option Pricing Model

The binomial model assumes the stock price can move to only two values over a single period: up (to Su) or down (to Sd). This allows us to find the replicating portfolio analytically.

One-Period Binomial: Call Pricing

Risk-neutral probability: p = (erΔt − d) / (u − d)

Call value: C = e−rΔt · [p · Cu + (1−p) · Cd]

where: u = up factor, d = down factor, r = risk-free rate,
Cu = max(Su − K, 0), Cd = max(Sd − K, 0)

Risk-neutral pricing: The probability p is not the "real" probability of the stock going up. It's the probability that makes the expected return on all assets equal to the risk-free rate in a world where risk is ignored. This mathematical trick lets us discount all payoffs at r.

Multi-Step Binomial Tree

Extending to multiple periods, the stock price follows a tree of possible paths. For a 2-period tree:

Nodes at T: S·u², S·ud, S·d²

Work backwards from terminal payoffs:
At each node: V = e−rΔt · [p·Vu + (1−p)·Vd]

For American options, compare to immediate exercise value at each node:
V = max(intrinsic value, continuation value)

The binomial tree converges to the Black-Scholes formula as the number of steps → ∞ (and Δt → 0). With 50+ steps, the binomial price is essentially indistinguishable from Black-Scholes for European options.

The Black-Scholes Model

Black and Scholes (1973) derived a closed-form solution for European option prices assuming the underlying follows geometric Brownian motion (continuous log-normal price paths).

C = S · N(d₁) − K · e−rT · N(d₂)    (Call)
P = K · e−rT · N(−d₂) − S · N(−d₁)    (Put)

d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)T] / (σ√T)
d₂ = d₁ − σ√T

N(·) = cumulative standard normal distribution function
σ = annual volatility of returns, T = time to expiry (years)
What N(d₁) and N(d₂) mean: N(d₂) = risk-neutral probability that the call finishes ITM at expiry. N(d₁) = delta of the call — the fraction of the underlying held in the replicating portfolio. S·N(d₁) is the "asset part" of the option; K·e^−rT·N(d₂) is the "payment part."
