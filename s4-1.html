Delta & Gamma

First and second-order price sensitivities — how option value changes as the underlying moves.

Delta (Δ): First-Order Price Sensitivity

Delta measures how much an option's price changes for a ₹1 change in the underlying asset price. It is simultaneously the option's hedge ratio and an approximation of the probability the option expires ITM.

Δ = ∂C / ∂S    (for a call)
Δ = ∂P / ∂S    (for a put)

BSM: Δcall = e−qT · N(d₁)    ∈ (0, 1)
BSM: Δput = −e−qT · N(−d₁) = Δcall − 1    ∈ (−1, 0)

Delta by Moneyness

MoneynessCall DeltaPut DeltaInterpretation
Deep ITM≈ +1.0≈ −1.0Moves like the underlying itself
ATM≈ +0.50≈ −0.5050% chance of expiring ITM
Deep OTM≈ 0≈ 0Very small sensitivity; low probability ITM
Delta as hedge ratio: If you are short one Nifty call with Δ = 0.4, buying 0.4 shares (or 0.4 of one Nifty futures contract) makes you delta-neutral. This is the core of delta hedging used by market makers.

Gamma (Γ): Second-Order Price Sensitivity

Gamma measures the rate of change of delta — how quickly the hedge ratio itself changes as the underlying moves. It is the second derivative of option price with respect to the underlying.

Γ = ∂Δ / ∂S = ∂²C / ∂S²

BSM: Γ = e−qT · N'(d₁) / (S · σ · √T)

where N'(d₁) = (1/√2π) · e−d₁²/2 is the standard normal PDF

Γ is identical for calls and puts (same strike, expiry)
Long Gamma (Option Buyers)
Γ > 0 for all long option positions (long call or long put).

Benefits from large moves in either direction — the delta shifts in your favour as the market moves.

Long gamma = long convexity of the payoff.
Short Gamma (Option Sellers)
Γ < 0 for all short option positions (short call or short put).

Hurt by large moves — delta shifts against you, making delta hedging expensive.

Short gamma = collect theta, but bleed on big moves.

Gamma by Moneyness and Time

ScenarioGammaImplication
ATM optionMaximumDelta changes fastest here — hedging most active
Deep ITM or OTMNear zeroDelta stable — low hedging need
Near expiry (ATM)Very highGamma risk spikes — "pin risk" near strike
Long time to expiryLowDelta changes slowly — easier to manage
Gamma-Theta trade-off: Long gamma (buying options) gives convexity — you benefit from large moves. But you pay for this with theta decay (time value erodes daily). Short gamma gives you theta income but bleeds on big moves. The market price of options reflects this exact trade-off.

Taylor Approximation: Δ and Γ Together

Using a second-order Taylor expansion, the change in option price for a change in spot can be approximated as:

ΔC ≈ Δ · ΔS + ½ · Γ · (ΔS)²

The Δ · ΔS term = first-order (linear) approximation
The ½ · Γ · (ΔS)² term = convexity correction (always positive for long options)

Worked Example

Given: Long call with Δ = 0.45, Γ = 0.008. Underlying moves from ₹2,460 to ₹2,510 (ΔS = +₹50).

ComponentCalculationValue
Delta component0.45 × 50₹22.50
Gamma component½ × 0.008 × 50²₹10.00
Total estimated ΔC22.50 + 10.00₹32.50

The gamma adds ₹10 of extra gain compared to the linear delta estimate. This is the benefit of long convexity — as the option moves ITM, delta increases, amplifying further gains.

Practice Problem

Problem 4.1: A call option has Δ = 0.60 and Γ = 0.012. The underlying drops by ₹30. Using the Taylor approximation, estimate the change in call price (in ₹). Positive = gain, negative = loss.

ΔC (₹)
Step-by-step:
ΔS = −30 (price fell)
Delta component: 0.60 × (−30) = −18.00
Gamma component: ½ × 0.012 × (−30)² = 0.006 × 900 = +5.40
Total ΔC ≈ −18.00 + 5.40 = −₹12.60

Note: gamma always adds a positive convexity correction (½·Γ·ΔS² is positive regardless of direction). The call lost ₹12.60 in value, not the ₹18 a naive delta estimate would suggest.

Quick Check

1. An ATM Nifty call option has a delta of approximately:

  • 0 — it has no intrinsic value
  • +0.50
  • +1.0 — it moves in lockstep with Nifty
  • −0.50

2. Where is Gamma highest for a given option series (same underlying, expiry)?

  • Deep ITM options
  • Deep OTM options
  • ATM (at-the-money) options
  • Gamma is constant across all strikes

3. In the Taylor approximation ΔC ≈ Δ·ΔS + ½·Γ·(ΔS)², the gamma term:

  • Is negative when the stock falls
  • Cancels out the delta term exactly
  • Is always positive for a long option position, regardless of direction
  • Only matters for very large price moves
