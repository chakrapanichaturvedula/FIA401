Futures Pricing

The cost-of-carry model, basis, contango vs backwardation, and arbitrage when prices deviate.

The Cost-of-Carry Model

The fair futures price F* is the no-arbitrage price derived from the cost of holding the underlying asset from now until expiry. Holding costs include financing (the risk-free rate r) minus any benefits received (dividends or convenience yield q).

F* = S · e(r − q)T     (continuous compounding)

F* = S · (1 + r − q)T    (simple compounding)

where: S = current spot price, r = risk-free rate (p.a.),
q = dividend/convenience yield (p.a.), T = time to expiry (years)
Financing Cost (r)
If you buy the underlying today instead of via futures, you must finance the purchase at the risk-free rate r. This cost pushes F* above S.
Benefits Received (q)
Dividends (equity), convenience yield (commodities), or storage-cost credits. These reduce the cost of carry and push F* below where it would otherwise be.
Nifty50 example: When r (≈6.5%) > q (≈1.5%), F* > S. Nifty futures are almost always in contango — futures trade above spot. Near expiry, F* converges to S because T → 0.

Basis

Basis = Spot price − Futures price. In India's cash market convention, basis is typically defined this way (cash minus futures). Basis can be positive (backwardation) or negative (contango).

Basis = S − F

At expiry: Basis → 0    (convergence)

Basis convergence is a fundamental property of futures markets. As T → 0, the futures price must converge to the spot price — otherwise there would be a riskless arbitrage at expiry. This convergence is the risk faced by hedgers when the futures contract doesn't expire exactly when their exposure ends.

Basis risk for hedgers: If you hedge using a futures contract that expires before or after your actual transaction date, the basis at the time you close your hedge may differ from the basis when you opened it. This residual uncertainty = basis risk.

Contango vs Backwardation

Contango (F > S)
Futures above spot. Normal for financial assets where financing costs exceed dividends.

Basis is negative (S − F < 0).
Price path of futures slopes upward through time.

Example: Nifty futures, most equity futures.
Backwardation (F < S)
Futures below spot. Occurs when convenience yield or dividends are high, or there's strong near-term demand.

Basis is positive (S − F > 0).

Examples: Crude oil in supply squeezes, dividend-heavy stocks before record date.

Cash-and-Carry Arbitrage

When the actual futures price deviates from F*, an arbitrageur can lock in a riskless profit by simultaneously taking positions in the spot and futures market.

SituationArbitrage ActionProfit
F > F*
(futures overpriced)		 ① Sell futures at F
② Buy spot at S
③ Borrow S at rate r		 F − F* (locked in at inception)

Deliver spot against futures at expiry
F < F*
(futures underpriced)		 ① Buy futures at F
② Short spot, receive S
③ Lend S at rate r		 F* − F (locked in at inception)

Receive spot at expiry to close short
Why this works: At expiry, futures converge to spot. If you sold futures at F and bought spot at S (financed at r), your net cost was F*. Since you contracted to deliver at F, your profit = F − F* regardless of where the market goes.
