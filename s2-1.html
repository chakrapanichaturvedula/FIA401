Futures Mechanics

How futures contracts work: forwards vs futures, open interest, daily MTM settlement, and the margin system.

Forwards vs Futures

A forward contract is a private agreement to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price on a future date. A futures contract achieves the same economic goal but is traded on an organised exchange with several key structural differences.

FeatureForwardFutures
Trading venueOTC (bilateral)Exchange (NSE, CME)
Contract designFully customisedStandardised (lot, expiry, underlying)
SettlementSingle settlement at maturityDaily MTM + final settlement
Counterparty riskHigh — bilateral credit exposureEliminated by CCP (NSCCL)
MarginNone (or negotiated)Initial + maintenance margin required
LiquidityLow — can't easily exitHigh — can close with offsetting trade
Price transparencyPrivatePublic, real-time on exchange
Typical usersCorporates hedging FX/commodityAll participants — corporate, retail, institution
In India: USDINR forwards are the most common OTC derivative — banks offer them to importers/exporters. USDINR futures on NSE are the exchange-traded equivalent with smaller lot sizes (USD 1,000) accessible to retail participants.

Open Interest (OI)

Open Interest is the total number of outstanding futures contracts that have not been settled or closed. It measures the depth of participation in the market — how many active positions exist.

OI Increases when…
A new buyer (long) AND a new seller (short) both open fresh positions. New money enters the market.
OI Decreases when…
An existing long AND an existing short both close their positions. Money exits the market.
TradeBuyerSellerOI Change
New long vs new shortOpens newOpens new+1
New long vs old long closingOpens newCloses existingNo change
Old short closing vs old long closingCloses existingCloses existing−1
OI ≠ Volume. Volume counts every contract traded today (buy + sell). OI counts only contracts still open at end of day. High volume + rising OI = strong new conviction. High volume + falling OI = active unwinding.

OI as a Trend Confirmation Tool

PriceOIInterpretation
Rising ↑Rising ↑Bullish — new longs entering, trend has conviction
Rising ↑Falling ↓Caution — short covering rally, may not sustain
Falling ↓Rising ↑Bearish — new shorts entering, downtrend has conviction
Falling ↓Falling ↓Longs liquidating, trend may be ending

The Margin System

To ensure traders can meet their obligations, exchanges require margins — deposits that act as performance bonds.

Initial Margin
Deposited when opening a position. Set as a % of contract value (typically 5–15% on NSE). Covers potential 1-day loss with high confidence (99% VaR).
Maintenance Margin
Minimum balance that must be maintained. If the margin account falls below this level, a margin call is triggered. Typically 75% of initial margin.
Variation Margin
The top-up payment required when a margin call is triggered. Must bring account back to the initial margin level (not just maintenance).
Daily MTM P&L = (Ft − Ft−1) × lot size × number of contracts

Margin call triggered when: Margin balance < Maintenance margin
Variation margin = Initial margin − Current balance

Worked Example: Daily MTM Settlement

Setup: Vikram buys 1 Nifty50 futures contract at ₹22,000. Lot size = 50. Initial margin = ₹1,20,000. Maintenance margin = ₹90,000.

DayClosing PriceDaily MTMCumulative P&LMargin BalanceAction
0 (entry)₹22,000—₹0 ₹1,20,000Position opened
1₹21,800 −₹10,000−₹10,000 ₹1,10,000—
2₹21,600 −₹10,000−₹20,000 ₹1,00,000—
3₹21,400 −₹10,000−₹30,000 ₹90,000 Margin call! Top up ₹30,000
4 (after top-up)₹21,700 +₹15,000−₹15,000 ₹1,35,000—

Day 1 MTM: (21,800 − 22,000) × 50 = −200 × 50 = −₹10,000. If Vikram cannot meet the margin call on Day 3, the broker will force-close his position.

Key insight: Daily MTM prevents large credit exposures from accumulating. Each day's loss is settled that night. The maximum credit risk at any point is roughly one day's price move — not the cumulative loss since inception.

Practice Problem

Problem 2.1: You buy 2 Nifty50 futures contracts at ₹22,500 (lot size = 50). The next day the contract closes at ₹22,350. What is your total MTM loss (in ₹)?

MTM Loss (₹)
Step-by-step:
Price change per unit = 22,350 − 22,500 = −150
MTM = −150 × 50 (lot size) × 2 (contracts)
= −150 × 100 = −₹15,000

Enter 15000 (the magnitude of the loss). This amount is debited from your margin account that evening.
