Hedging Strategies

Minimum variance hedge ratio, beta management of stock portfolios, and rolling hedges forward.

Why Not Hedge 1-for-1?

When the underlying exposure and the futures contract are different assets — or even the same asset with imperfect correlation — a naive 1:1 hedge will leave residual risk. The minimum variance hedge ratio (MVHR) finds the exact fraction of the exposure to hedge that minimises the variance of the hedged portfolio's value.

OLS regression insight: Regress ΔS (changes in spot price) on ΔF (changes in futures price): ΔS = α + h*·ΔF + ε. The slope coefficient h* is the optimal hedge ratio. The R² of this regression = hedge effectiveness — how much of the spot price variance is explained by the futures.

Minimum Variance Hedge Ratio

h* = ρ · (σS / σF)

where:
ρ = correlation between ΔS and ΔF
σS = standard deviation of changes in spot price
σF = standard deviation of changes in futures price
When h* < 1
Futures are more volatile than spot, or correlation is less than 1. Hedge less than 100% of exposure. Useful in cross-hedges (different but related assets).
When h* = 1 (perfect hedge)
ρ = 1 and σS = σF. The futures perfectly tracks the spot — one futures contract hedges exactly one unit of exposure.

Number of Contracts

N* = h* × (QA / QF)

QA = size of position being hedged (units)
QF = size of one futures contract (units)

Worked Example

Given: A refinery needs to hedge 1,00,000 barrels of crude oil using crude futures (contract = 1,000 barrels). From historical data: ρ = 0.92, σS = 2.8 USD/barrel, σF = 3.1 USD/barrel.

StepCalculationResult
MVHRh* = 0.92 × (2.8 / 3.1)h* = 0.831
Contracts neededN* = 0.831 × (1,00,000 / 1,000)83 contracts

The refinery shorts 83 futures contracts (not 100) because the futures are more volatile than the spot. Hedging 100 contracts would over-hedge and actually introduce more variance.

Stock Portfolio Hedging: Beta Management

A fund manager with a diversified equity portfolio can use index futures to adjust the portfolio's market sensitivity (beta) without selling individual stocks.

N* = (βtarget − βportfolio) × P / (F × A)

where:
βportfolio = current portfolio beta
βtarget = desired beta (0 = fully hedged, market neutral)
P = portfolio market value (₹)
F = current index futures price
A = index multiplier (lot size)

Positive N* → Buy contracts (increase beta)
Negative N* → Sell contracts (reduce beta)

Worked Example: Nifty Portfolio Hedge

Setup: Portfolio value P = ₹2,50,00,000. Portfolio β = 1.3. Target β = 0 (full hedge). Nifty futures price F = 22,000. Lot size A = 50.

StepCalculationResult
Contract valueF × A = 22,000 × 50₹11,00,000
N* (contracts to sell)(0 − 1.3) × 2,50,00,000 / 11,00,000−29.5 → Sell 30 contracts
Reducing Beta (Defensive)
Bear market expected. Sell Nifty futures to reduce β → portfolio less sensitive to market decline. βtarget < βportfolio → Sell contracts.
Increasing Beta (Aggressive)
Bull market expected. Buy Nifty futures to increase β → portfolio more sensitive to market rise. βtarget > βportfolio → Buy contracts.

Rolling the Hedge Forward

When the hedging horizon extends beyond the nearest futures expiry date, you cannot maintain a single contract to the end. You must roll the hedge: close the near-month contract just before expiry and open the next-month contract.

DateActionWhy
Month 1Short near-month contractInitiate hedge
Near expiryBuy back near-month; sell next-monthRoll forward
Month 2 expiryBuy back Month 2; sell Month 3Roll again if needed
Final dateClose final contractComplete hedge
Roll risk: Each roll introduces a new basis (the difference between near and far contract prices). If the far contract is significantly in contango, rolling costs money. This "roll cost" can erode the hedge benefit over time. Stack-and-roll strategies used by airlines (fuel hedges) often span 12–18 months and face significant roll costs.

Practice Problem

Problem 2.4: A fund has a portfolio worth ₹1,50,00,000 with β = 1.2. The manager wants to reduce β to 0 (fully hedge against market). Nifty futures are trading at ₹21,500 with lot size = 50. How many contracts should the manager sell?

Contracts to sell
Step-by-step:
Contract value = 21,500 × 50 = ₹10,75,000
N* = (βtarget − βP) × P / (F × A)
= (0 − 1.2) × 1,50,00,000 / 10,75,000
= −1.2 × 13.95 = −16.74
Round to 17 contracts short (sell 17)

Quick Check

1. The minimum variance hedge ratio h* is calculated as:

  • σS / σF (volatility ratio only)
  • ρ × (σF / σS)
  • ρ × (σS / σF)
  • ρ² × (σS / σF)

2. An R² of 0.90 in the hedge ratio regression means:

  • The optimal hedge ratio is exactly 0.90
  • 90% of the spot price variance is explained by futures price variance
  • The hedge eliminates 90% of all risk
  • The hedge ratio is 10% too large

3. "Rolling a hedge" in futures markets means:

  • Closing the position early for a guaranteed profit
  • Switching from futures to options to reduce cost
  • Closing the expiring contract and opening the next-month contract
  • Changing the number of contracts to match a new exposure
